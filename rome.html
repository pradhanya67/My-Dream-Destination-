ROME

ABOUT:

>Rome's history spans 28 centuries. While Roman mythology dates the founding of Rome at around 753 BC,
the site has been inhabited for much longer, making it a major human settlement for over three millennia
and one of the oldest continuously occupied cities in Europe The city's early population originated from
a mix of Latins, Etruscans, and Sabines. Eventually, the city successively became the capital of the
Roman Kingdom, the Roman Republic and the Roman Empire, and is regarded by many as the first-ever
Imperial city and metropolis. It was first called The Eternal City (Latin: Urbs Aeterna; Italian:
La Città Eterna) by the Roman poet Tibullus in the 1st century BC, and the expression was also taken
up by Ovid, Virgil, and Livy. Rome is also called Caput Mundi (Capital of the World).

After the fall of the Empire in the west, which marked the beginning of the Middle Ages, Rome slowly fell under
the political control of the Papacy, and in the 8th century, it became the capital of the Papal States, which
lasted until 1870. Beginning with the Renaissance, almost all popes since Nicholas V (1447–1455) pursued a
coherent architectural and urban programme over four hundred years, aimed at making the city the artistic and
cultural centre of the world. In this way, Rome first became one of the major centres of the Renaissance
and then became the birthplace of both the Baroque style and Neoclassicism. Famous artists, painters, sculptors,
and architects made Rome the centre of their activity, creating masterpieces throughout the city. In 1871, Rome
became the capital of the Kingdom of Italy, which, in 1946, became the Italian Republic.

In 2019, Rome was the 14th most visited city in the world, with 8.6 million tourists, the third most visited city in
the European Union, and the most popular tourist destination in Italy. Its historic centre is listed by UNESCO
as a World Heritage Site. The host city for the 1960 Summer Olympics, Rome is also the seat of several specialised
agencies of the United Nations, such as the Food and Agriculture Organization, World Food Programme, International
Fund for Agricultural Development and UN System Network on Rural Development and Food Security. The city also hosts
the European Union (EU) Delegation to the United Nations (UN), Secretariat of the Parliamentary Assembly of the Union
for the Mediterranean, headquarters of the World Farmers' Organisation, multi-country office of the United Nations
High Commissioner for Refugees, Human Resources Office for International Cooperation of the United Nations Department
of Economic and Social Affairs, headquarters of the International Labour Organization Office for Italy, headquarters
of the WORLD BANK GROUP for Italy, Office for Technology Promotion and Investment in Italy under the United Nations
Industrial Development Organization, Rome office of the United Nations Interregional Crime and Justice Research Institute
, and support office of the United Nations Humanitarian Response Depot, as well as the headquarters of several Italian
multinational companies such as Eni, Enel, TIM, Leonardo, and banks such as BNL. Numerous companies are based within
Rome's EUR business district, such as the luxury fashion house Fendi located in the Palazzo della Civiltà Italiana. The presence of renowned international brands in the city has made Rome an important centre of fashion and design,
and the Cinecittà Studios have been the set of many Academy Award–winning movies.

FOOD:

Rome offers a rich culinary scene with many must-try dishes. Some of the most popular include Cacio e pepe, Pasta alla Gricia,
Pasta all'Amatriciana, and Pasta alla Carbonara, Saltimbocca alla Romana, Carciofi alla Romana and Giudia, Coda alla Vaccinara,
and Trapizzino. Street food options like Pizza al Taglio and Supplì are also popular.

Pasta Dishes:

Cacio e pepe:

A simple yet flavorful dish featuring pasta (traditionally tonnarelli or spaghetti), pecorino romano cheese, and black pepper.

Pasta alla Gricia:

A classic Roman pasta dish made with guanciale (cured pork cheek), pecorino romano cheese, and black pepper.

Pasta all'Amatriciana:

Another Roman favorite, this dish includes bucatini pasta, guanciale, tomato sauce, and pecorino romano cheese.

Pasta alla Carbonara:

A rich and creamy pasta dish made with spaghetti, guanciale, eggs, pecorino romano cheese, and black pepper.

Other Roman Specialties:

Saltimbocca alla Romana:

Thin slices of veal topped with prosciutto and sage, then sauteed in butter and white wine.

Carciofi alla Romana:

Artichokes cooked in olive oil, garlic, and mint, a quintessential Roman dish.

Carciofi alla Giudia:

Artichokes deep-fried until crispy and golden brown.

Coda alla Vaccinara:

Braised oxtail in a rich tomato sauce, a hearty Roman stew.

Trapizzino:

: A triangular pocket of pizza bianca filled with various savory ingredients, a popular street food.

Supplì:

Fried rice balls, often containing tomato sauce and mozzarella, a beloved Roman street food.

Porchetta:

Roasted pork, often served in sandwiches.

Filetti di Baccalà:

Fried cod fillets, a popular Roman street food.

Other Notable Foods:

Pizza al Taglio:

Pizza sold by the slice, a popular Roman street food.

Maritozzo:

A sweet bun filled with whipped cream, a traditional Roman pastry.

Gelato:

Italian ice cream, a must-try in Rome.

Tiramisu:

A classic Italian dessert, also enjoyed in Rome.

WHAT ROME IS FAMOUS FOR :

Rome is famous for its rich history, iconic landmarks, and cultural significance. It's often called the "Eternal City"
due to its ancient ruins like the Colosseum and Roman Forum, which are testaments to the Roman Empire. The city is also
a center of art, particularly during the Renaissance, and is home to Vatican City, the heart of the Catholic Church.
Rome is also known for its delicious cuisine, including pizza al taglio (pizza by the slice) and pasta dishes.

Ancient Rome:

Colosseum:

The largest amphitheater ever built, capable of holding 80,000 spectators, it was a venue for gladiatorial contests and public spectacles.

Roman Forum:

The heart of ancient Rome, it was once a bustling hub of temples, basilicas, and government buildings.

Pantheon:

An ancient Roman temple, known for its impressive dome and oculus.

Palatine Hill:

One of the seven hills of Rome, believed to be the location where Romulus founded the city.

Ancient Roman culture:

Ancient Rome's contributions to language, religion, law, government, art, architecture, and engineering continue to influence the modern world.

Vatican City:

St. Peter's Basilica:

A masterpiece of Renaissance architecture and the largest church ever built, located within Vatican City.

Sistine Chapel:

Home to Michelangelo's famous frescoes, including the ceiling and the Last Judgment.

Vatican Museums:

Home to a vast collection of art and historical artifacts, including the Vatican Library.

Other Notable Aspects:

Trevi Fountain:

A baroque fountain, famous for the tradition of throwing coins into its waters.

Piazza Navona:

A vibrant public square with three elaborate fountains, baroque architecture, and a lively atmosphere.

Renaissance Art and Architecture:

Rome is a treasure trove of Renaissance art and architecture, with numerous churches, palaces, and museums showcasing masterpieces from this period.

Culinary Scene:

Rome is known for its delicious food, including pizza al taglio (rectangular slices of pizza with various toppings) and classic pasta dishes like
cacio e pepe and carbonara.

Gelato:

Rome is also famous for its gelato, a creamy Italian ice cream that comes in a wide array of flavors.
